Of all the misadventures, none was quite so spectacular—or so painful—as the incident at the kindergarten. One evening, as twilight shadowed the road winding around my old kindergarten, I cannot now recall where I was headed or what occupied my mind, but I do remember the precise moment when impulse seized control of reason. A group of girls had gathered ahead of me, and for reasons I still cannot explain, I decided to run directly toward them. Perhaps I wished to startle them, to prove something, or simply to inject some excitement into an otherwise unremarkable evening. The girls spotted me approaching at full speed, and their reaction was immediate and unanimous: they fled in a mass panic. Something in their fear—perhaps the thrill of their unexpected reaction, perhaps the absurdity of the situation—was deeply exciting. I became increasingly absorbed in the chase, my excitement building until I could think of nothing other than catching up with them in their panicked flight.

In my single-minded focus on finding which direction they had fled, I failed to notice a treacherous obstacle lurking in the dimming light, a thin piece of metal stretched between two poles, the kind of tumble bar that seemed almost invisible against the gathering shadows of twilight. I crashed into it headfirst, the metal connecting squarely with my upper teeth. The impact was sickening, a crash of metal against bone that sent me sprawling to the ground, half-conscious from the impact, until the world stopped spinning enough for me to stumble back home. The tooth was cracked but somehow remained in its socket—a small mercy that did little to dull the pain. I hid beneath a desk in my room and wept for what felt like an eternity, equal parts physical pain and emotional devastation. Perhaps the most remarkable finale to this whole affair was that one of the older girls, showing more compassion than my dramatic exit warranted, later returned to the scene and brought back my hat that I had left behind in the chaos.

Yesterday contained something I might call a coming out, though it felt too quiet and too small to deserve such a heavy name. With the house empty during the day—a rare occurrence, given that only one roommate remains with us while others have scattered to distant places or left this world altogether—I finally had the privacy to explore an aspect of myself that had been waiting patiently in the shadows.

I began with the simple rituals of preparation—a long, soothing bath that felt like a cleanse not just of skin but of spirit. The depilation spray, which I had been saving for some imagined future occasion, had run out at the worst possible moment, but I made do with what remained, smoothing cream over skin that had never known such attention. And then—the most daring part—I allowed myself to get dressed. I slipped into clothing that had been hidden away, placed a wig upon my head, and applied makeup. The makeup was rushed, unpracticed, and certainly imperfect, yet when I finally caught my glimpse in the mirror, the reflection staring back at me was not the monster I had feared. In fact, it was rather beautiful. For the first time in longer than I can remember, I felt something stir within me—not shame, but genuine desire to be seen, to exist openly. I even felt a flicker of desire to step outside, to show this new look to others, to be seen. I walked around the apartment, pausing to take awkward photographs, feeling something that had long been dormant bloom back to life.

But as afternoon bled toward evening, the housemate returned, and the spell began to fracture. Their initial reactions were warm, even complimentary—I could see genuine pleasure in their eyes as they took in my appearance. Yet that warmth curdled quickly into something darker. Warnings tumbled forth: this behavior could bring trouble, this expression was risky, this version of me was dangerous to reveal to a world that might not understand. Their voices grew more urgent, weaving paranoid theories about how others might react, what consequences might follow, how I was opening myself to harm. What had been a celebration of self became an inquisition of fear.

Their concern, however well-intentioned, felt suffocating. More troubling still, I found myself worrying about them—the wildness of their theories, the intensity of their fear on my behalf, the way their thoughts spiraled into patterns that seemed to originate more from their own anxieties than from any realistic assessment of my situation. I stood there, still adorned in my wig and makeup, watching people I cared about unravel into panicked speculation, and I felt the joy I had cultivated all day slowly leaking away like air from a punctured balloon.

Yet here is what I have learned about reaching rock bottom: once you have already arrived at the lowest point, there is nowhere further to fall. Their words wounded me, certainly—they always do, these reminders that the world is less forgiving than I wish it to be—but they couldn't undo what I had already done. The delight of that afternoon, the brief and beautiful moment of seeing myself clearly in the mirror, remains mine. It happened. It was real.
